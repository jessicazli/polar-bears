Polar Bears in a Warming World

Things are heating up in the arctic...

A Closer Look into Climate Change

Arctic sea ice has overall decreased over the years, due global warming, which has resulted from detriments such as increased carbon dioxide emissions.

But what do rising temperatures and decreasing ice mass mean for polar bears?

19 Subpopulations throughout the Arctic in 2021

How did the melting of the Arctic sea ice affect polar bears in each of the 19 different subregions in year 2021?

Majority of subregions experience sea ice loss in 2021, with Norweigan Sea losing the largest percentage (26.5%) of sea ice extent per decade between 1979 and 2019 among all Arctic regions. This region records the second-lowest polar bear population of 203 bears in 2021. Chukchi Sea lost the second largest sea ice extent with 25%, which is alarming because this region also has the largest polar bear population.

Population change data is unknown or deficient for most subpopulations, but among the subpopulations with information available, 3 of those populations likely decreased.


Where did we get this data from?

The data comes from The IUCN Polar Bear Specialist Group report and Statista.

Migration Towards Land

Over the last 30-40 years, polar bears in the Chukchi and Southern Beaufort Sea regions have been migrating further and further south, away from the center of the arctic ice circle and towards land (Alaska, Canada) due to the decreasing ice extent.

How do we know this?

Scientists have been tracking polar bears by placing radio collars on them. The collars emit signals that can be picked up by satellites, which allow scientists to track the polar bears' movements.

Southern Beaufort Sea Polar Bear Diet Trend

Naturally, polar bears are adapted to live on sea ice, and they rely on sea ice to hunt seals, their main food source. As sea ice melts, polar bears must adapt to changes in their environment. Population trends from 2021 show that Southern Beaufort Sea polar bear populations have likely decreased. Specifically, let's see how the diets of Southern Beaufort Sea polar bears have changed between 2004-2016.

As sea ice extent decreased throughout the years, there was a significant decline in polar bears' consumption of Beluga whales from 2004 to 2016. Polar Bears are carnivores and feed mainly on ringed and beared seals. Depending on their location and available food sources, they also hunt whales. The energy contained in whales is more than seals. According to scientists Laidre et al., a population of polar bears would need to eat more than 1,300 adult ringed seals to obtain the same amount of energy stored in one bowhead whale carcass. This difference in polar bear diet throughout the years indicates their changing environment and the potential impact of climate change on other arctic species like whales.

We accessed the dataset from USGS Alaska Science Center - Polar Bear Research Program. To obtain the percentage breakdown by diet type in a particular year, we calculated the total consumption of each food source from that year and divided by the number of polar bears captured. This yields the average consumption (as % of total diet) of each food source.
